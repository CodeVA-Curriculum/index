Teachable Machine

Summary

In this lesson, students explore the basics of artificial intelligence (AI) and (ML), focusing on the biases that can arise in AI models and the ethical considerations involved in their development and use. Through hands-on activities using Teachable Machine, students will create their own AI models, analyze the biases present, and discuss strategies to mitigate these biases. The lesson emphasizes the importance of fairness and justice in AI systems and encourages students to reflect on the societal impact of biased AI.

Objectives

The students will be able to . . .

  • Give a basic, high-level explanation of how artificial intelligence and machine learning works.
  • Create a machine learning model using Teachable Machine.
  • Identify and analyze biases present in AI models.
  • Evaluate the ethical considerations and justice issues involved in developing and using ML models.
  • Discuss strategies to mitigate biases in AI models.
  • Reflect on the societal impact of biased AI systems.

Vocabulary

Term

Definition

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems.

Machine Learning (ML)

A subset of AI that involves the use of algorithms and statistical models to enable computers to improve their performance on tasks through experience.

Algorithm

A set of rules or processes to be followed in problem-solving operations, often used by computers.

Bias

A systematic error introduced into sampling or testing by selecting or encouraging one outcome or answer over others.

Training data or dataset

The dataset used to train an AI model to recognize patterns or make decisions.

Model

In AI, a model is a representation of what a machine learning system has learned from the training data.

Ethics

Moral principles that govern a person's behavior or conducting an activity, especially concerning the fairness and justice of decisions.

Prerequisite Knowledge

This lesson does not require any prerequisite computer science knowledge on the part of students. It is a beginner-friendly introduction to AI and bias. Students should have a basic understanding of bias and its role in societal/systemic marginalization and oppression.

Day 1 Outline                                                                   Formative Assessment Notes

1.

Warm-Up: AI Discussion? Facilitate a discussion where students share their prior conceptions of AI/machine learning. Here are some prompts for students to reflect on::

  • How would you define AI to a friend/family member?
  • What are some examples of AI that you use in your daily life?
  • How do you know something is AI or not?

Explain that AI has two important attributes; 1) a dataset used as the basis for its behavior, and 2) an algorithm that takes new data as input and outputs a prediction.

Students need to understand AI has 2 main attributes: a dataset, and an algorithm that governs how the computer makes predictions given new data. The next activity provides an opportunity to check students’ understanding of this concept.

2.

Activity: AI or Not? Pull up the image linked here and drag and drop the icons into “AI” or “Not AI” columns. Model how to put icons into the categories, then prompt students to finish categorizing all of the icons individually, as a class, or in groups of 2-3.

After students have finished categorizing the icons, facilitate a brief discussion. Here are some prompts:

  • Pick an icon that you were really sure about—how did you know that the icon belonged in the category you chose for it?
  • Were there any icons you weren’t sure about?

Be sure to provide feedback as students discuss their categorization decisions. Remind them of the two main attributes of AI that they should focus on for the purposes of this lesson.

3.

Meep or Not: As a whole group, go through the creature deck to predict whether each creature is a “meep” or “not a meep.” Very little guidance should be given for why a specific creature belongs in a category.

  1. Present creature image on slide deck.
  2. Allow a few moments to predict whether or not the creature could be identified as a “meep.”
  3. Reveal whether the creature is really a meep or not (answers are animated on slide deck).
  4. Allow a brief processing time and discussion (also allowing for physically putting the image in the correct category) before presenting the next creature.

Debrief by facilitating a discussion with students where they reflect on how they categorized the images. Here are some prompts:

  • How did you decide whether an item was a meep or not? similarities/differences? List of rules? Guessing?
  • Show the students how the meeps are categorized. What other types of data get sorted this way? What problems might arise from sorting data this way?

4.

Introduction to Teachable Machine: Guide students to teachable machine website 

Model how to label classes, collect data and train the model. You can create models using images, audio or poses. Demonstrating the process using images first is helpful (for example, create a model that can tell the difference between a pencil and a marker). Here are some important ideas to emphasize during this demonstration:

  • The first thing to do is provide Teachable Machine with a dataset in the form of images (or sound, or poses)
  • Next, “train” the model—in this step, the computer creates an algorithm (also called a model) to make predictions with new data.
  • Testing the algorithm involves providing it with new images (or sounds, or poses) and seeing if it makes correct predictions with this new data.

It’s a good idea to invite students to participate in this demonstration by asking questions and making suggestions.

5.

Create AI Models with Teachable Machine: Have the students break into small groups to create their own models. Here are some prompts students might address with their models:

  • Create a model that identifies different types of dance moves using body poses.
  • Create a model that distinguishes between various emotions using facial expressions.
  • Create a model that classifies different types of animals using pictures.
  • Create a model that recognizes different hand gestures.
  • Create a model that differentiates between various musical instruments based on sounds.

Alternatively, you can have students come up with their own ideas for predictive models. Here are some prompts to start their brainstorming conversations (note that this takes a little bit longer):

  • How could AI be used to assist in education?
  • Can you think of a model that would help in environmental conservation?
  • How could AI be used to enhance creativity in arts or music? What would your model focus on?
  • What safety or security applications could benefit from AI? What type of model would you develop for this purpose?
  • Is there someone in your life or community that could benefit from a model you create?

During this activity, float around the room and make sure students understand how to operate the website.

6.

Testing Teachable Machine Models: Have the groups to go test other group’s models. Have students reflect on the following discussion prompts as they do their testing:

  • How well does the model work?
  • When does the model fail? Can you make it give a bad prediction?
  • Is there a pattern you can see in the model’s good and bad predictions?

 

Facilitate a discussion about students’ results after testing. Bring special attention to failure patterns—models will often fail to work well in many situations. For example, a model designed to identify if someone is happy or sad will fail to make accurate predictions for users with a dark complexion if it was trained on light-skinned faces.

This is the most crucial moment in the lesson. You want students to see that their models are successful when they are expected to make predictions based on a diverse set of data. You will use this discovery as the basis for a conversation about how AI technologies might be biased or cause harm in real life.

7.

Exit Ticket: Have students reflect individually in writing on the following prompt:

How well did your model work during testing? Were there any big issues to address? How do you think you could improve your model?

You can find printable versions of this exit ticket below.

We aren’t looking for a huge clarity of thought here–we just want students to consider how they might shape their model in the next class.

Day 2 Outline                                                                   Formative Assessment Notes

8.

Refining AI Models: Optionally, have students attempt to improve their model by adding more data and re-training. They should use their reflections from the prior day’s exit ticket to inform these revisions.

Prompt students to think carefully about what kind of data their model might be missing.

9.

Summative Assessment: Have students complete one or more of the summative assessment activities below (see Assessment Strategies for details):

  • Bias in AI Group Presentations: Students present about the bias they identified in their models and how they attempted to address it.
  • Ethical Considerations Debate: Students argue for and against the use of AI

Assessment Strategies

In addition to formative assessments (see Outline above), here are some summative opportunities:

Bias in AI Presentations

  1. Group Presentations: Have each group present their observations on the biases they discovered in their AI models or the ones they tested.
  2. Whole Group Discussion: Allow time for students to consider these questions independently and then with their groups:
  • Types of Bias: What kinds of bias does your model contain? (E.g., gender bias, racial bias, environmental bias)
  • Societal Implications: How does your model’s bias align with or contradict broader societal biases (e.g., racism, sexism)?
  • Mitigation Strategies: Discuss potential strategies for mitigating bias in AI models. What could be done to ensure the model works fairly across different contexts?
  • Ethical Considerations: Why is it important to address bias in AI models? Discuss the ethical implications of deploying biased AI systems in the real world.

Ethical Considerations Scenario

  1. Introduction
  • Briefly explain that students will explore a scenario and create potential solutions to ethical dilemmas in AI.
  1. Scenario Exploration
  • Split the class into small groups (3-4 members).
  • Provide each group with the scenario and discussion questions: Healthcare Ethical Scenario
  1. Presentation and Reflection
  • Each group presents their discussion points and potential solutions to the class.
  • Facilitate a class discussion encouraging students to compare and contrast the ethical issues and solutions proposed by each group.
  1. Individual Reflection
  • Exit Ticket: Have students complete the following reflection questions:
  • Write a paragraph on what you learned from exploring this ethical scenario and how it influenced your understanding of ethical considerations in AI.
  • How can you apply what you've learned about AI ethics in your daily life or future career?

  1. Assessment Checklist:

  • Provided a basic, high-level explanation of how artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) works.
  • Successfully demonstrated understanding about  the model's function and purpose.
  • Identified and articulated biases present in AI models during scenario discussions.
  • Proposed thoughtful steps to mitigate biases in AI models during group discussions and presentations.
  • Provided examples of how transparency and accountability can be ensured in AI development.
  • Completed the individual written reflection on the societal impact of biased AI systems.

Accommodations & Extensions

Here are some suggested accommodations. The following list is not exhaustive and is adaptable for your particular context.

Accommodations:

Students learning English as a second language:

  • ESL Resources: 
  • Provide WIDA resources
  • Reading:
  • Use sentence stems or fill-in-the-blank to answer reading comprehension questions.
  • Provide translated materials and instructions when needed.
  • Use visuals and demonstrations to support understanding.
  • Writing:
  • Allow students to be broader in their understanding of concepts.
  • Provide cards with pictures to help demonstrate understanding.
  • Simplify language without diluting content.
  • Speaking:
  • Allow students to simplify their answers and give extra time to think about and respond to questions.
  • Pair ESL students with bilingual peers to aid comprehension and participation.

Students with Cognitive/Learning Disabilities

  • Reading and Writing:
  • Provide extended time to read and write, including providing activity materials to prepare in advance.
  • Provide clear, step-by-step written and verbal instructions.
  • Use sentence stems or fill-in-the-blank questions for written responses.
  • Allow students to use drawings instead of written words to express their understanding.
  • If possible, allow students to work in a small group.
  • Discussions:
  • Give ample wait time when asking questions.
  • Write down questions and provide them to students before engaging with activities.

Students with Physical Disabilities

  • Adapted Activities:
  • Use adaptive technology as needed (e.g., screen readers, voice-to-text software).
  • For physical activities, use larger objects that are easier to manipulate or provide support as needed
  • For digital activities, ensure content is compatible with assistive technologies.
  • Allow frequent breaks to help maintain focus and reduce fatigue.

Extensions:

  • Option 1: Advanced AI Model
  • Have students develop more complex AI models using additional platforms, focusing on reducing bias through diverse and balanced datasets.
  • Option 2: Research Project
  • Assign students to research and present on real-world instances of AI bias and their societal impact.
